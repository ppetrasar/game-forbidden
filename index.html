UNRAVELLING THE RULES

To win the game, be the first to publish 21 papers and claim the ultimate academic glory: the Nobel Prize (but beware, someone may be trying to steal it...)! How to get these papers? Brace yourself and read on.

1. FIND OUT WHICH SHOLAR YOU ARE...

At the start of the game, you’ll receive a Scholar Card telling you which of six legendary scholars you are. Each scholar has a secret mission. Complete yours successfully, and you’ll earn a glorious 10 published papers (just imagine how many citations you’ll get...).

2. OBSERVE, CONFRONT, BLUFF

As the game progresses, closely observe your fellow players. Based on their actions, you might figure out which secret scholar they are. If you're confident in your skills, confront them: I think you are scholar...

If you're right: sweet, sweet academic validation. You get five published papers, and they suffer ultimate academic shame... 10 of their papers get retracted! However, if you are wrong... Oof... Five of your papers get retracted, and your opponent publishes five new papers (in Q1 journals... Maybe even Nature or Science... Just so it hurts you more)

Note for Thee: Bluff! Don't just sit there being honest. Bluff like the p- value depends on it. Pretend you're a different scholar. Confuse your enemies.

SCHOLARS

Behold! Before you stand some of the most iconic scholars, each with their own secret mission. Read carefully. Fulfil their bizarre academic desires, and you shall be ever closer to victory...

3. DISCUSS

First, begin by reading a question aloud to your fellow intellectuals. Without discussing, each player has to write down their answer. Keep it simple , for example write 'Yes' or 'No'.

In the moment everyone has written down their profound judgements, reveal them simultaneously. If there is a clear majority (e.g., 3 out of 4 players give the same answer), all players in the majority earn one published paper. If answers are split equally, no published papers are awarded. If all players agree upon the same answer, each player earns 1 published paper, and you're all thriving in the lazy warmth of groupthink.

Granted that at least one bold rebel challenged the majority, a discussion phase begins.

Here, you have up to three minutes to battle for the supremacy of your argument.

Then, after the discussion, vote again: if all players agree during the second vote, each player earns one published paper. Hooray for the mob mentality! If there's still disagreement, nobody gets a published paper, and (mirroring the classic p>0.05 scenario) you all continue intellectually enriched, but slightly annoyed!

SOME QUESTIONS FOR THEE

Should cases of scientific fraud- where researchers falsify, fabricate, or plagiarize data or their findings- be openly disclosed to the general public?

Though often criticised, traditional research metrics such as journal impact factor, citation counts and h-index, remain widely used to assess research impact. Should we ditch them entirely and replace them with alternative methods, or do they still have an important role?

Are Ethics Committees (IRBs) overprotective towards research participants?

You are doing a literature search for your paper and you come across work that you would like to cite it as support for your argument. You notice that the author of the paper is someone who got committed for sexual harassment. Do you cite their work?

Do you think open science currently benefits a narrow group of privileged researchers more than the broader scientific community?

Can there be something like voluntary participation in research with animals?

Is the risk of participants being identified from open datasets a serious enough concern to limit open data sharing?

Imagine there's a major crisis in the science, and you must choose one of three individuals- a philosopher, a politician, or a scientist- to address and resolve the issue. Who would you choose?

You are a researcher doing empirical work with adult participants. Your University just had an ethical issue where participants' data got known due to an error. Is this going to affect your career negatively more if you work at a big, famous University or a small, not famous University?
